How Pacemakers Work

The Basics

A pacemaker is essentially an artificial electrical system for your heart. It monitors your heart's natural rhythm and steps in when needed to ensure your heart maintains a healthy beat.

The Heart's Natural Electrical System

Your heart naturally generates electrical impulses that tell it when to beat. These impulses travel through the heart in a specific pattern, triggering contractions that pump blood. When this system doesn't work properly, a pacemaker takes over.

How a Pacemaker Monitors Your Heart

1

Sensing

The pacemaker constantly monitors your heart's electrical activity through the leads.

2

Analyzing

It analyzes whether your heart is beating at the right rate and rhythm.

3

Responding

If the heart rate is too slow or irregular, the pacemaker sends an electrical impulse to the heart muscle, triggering a heartbeat.

4

Adjusting

Modern pacemakers can adjust their rate based on your activity level and body's needs.

Programming & Settings

Your cardiologist can program your pacemaker to work in different modes:

  • Demand Mode: The device only paces when needed
  • Fixed Rate Mode: The device paces at a set rate regardless of natural activity
  • Rate-Adaptive Mode: Adjusts pacing rate based on physical activity and metabolic needs

The Role of Leads

Leads are flexible wires that connect the pacemaker to your heart. They deliver electrical impulses and sense your heart's natural activity. Most pacemakers have 1-3 leads depending on the type and what kind of pacing is needed.

Battery & Power

Pacemakers are powered by long-lasting batteries that typically last 5-15 years. Once the battery is depleted, the entire pacemaker needs to be replaced in a simple surgical procedure.
