What is a Pacemaker?

Definition

A pacemaker is a small, battery-powered medical device that helps maintain a regular heart rhythm. It's implanted under the skin, usually near the collarbone, and connected to the heart via thin wires called leads.

Why Do People Need Pacemakers?

Arrhythmia

When the heart beats too slowly (bradycardia), too quickly (tachycardia), or irregularly, a pacemaker can help regulate the rhythm.

Heart Block

A condition where electrical signals don't properly travel from the upper to lower chambers of the heart.

Sick Sinus Syndrome

When the heart's natural pacemaker (the sinus node) doesn't work properly.

Post-Surgery Recovery

Sometimes temporary pacemakers are used after heart surgery.

Key Components

Battery & Circuitry

Powers the device and controls the timing of electrical impulses. Modern batteries last 5-15 years.

Leads

Thin wires that carry electrical signals from the pacemaker to the heart and back.

Pulse Generator

Creates and delivers electrical pulses to keep the heart beating at the right rate.

Life with a Pacemaker

Most people with pacemakers can return to normal activities. Regular check-ups are important to ensure the device is working properly. Advanced pacemakers can even be monitored remotely!
