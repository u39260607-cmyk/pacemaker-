Types of Pacemakers

Overview

Pacemakers come in different types, each designed for specific heart conditions and patient needs. The choice depends on the type of arrhythmia and how much pacing support is required.

Single-Chamber Pacemakers

How They Work

Uses one lead, typically placed in the right ventricle (lower chamber). It paces only the lower chamber of the heart.

Best For:

  • Patients with bradycardia (slow heart rate)
  • Patients who don't need atrial pacing

Advantages:

  • Simpler procedure
  • Lower cost
  • Fewer complications

Dual-Chamber Pacemakers

How They Work

Uses two leads—one in the right atrium (upper chamber) and one in the right ventricle (lower chamber). Paces both chambers and maintains proper timing between them.

Best For:

  • Patients with AV block (heart block)
  • Patients who need coordination between chambers

Advantages:

  • More physiological pacing pattern
  • Better exercise tolerance
  • Improved quality of life

Biventricular Pacemakers (CRT)

How They Work

Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (CRT) devices use three leads to pace both ventricles and the atrium. This helps coordinate contractions in patients with heart failure.

Best For:

  • Patients with heart failure and irregular rhythm
  • Patients needing better heart synchronization

Advantages:

  • Improves heart pumping efficiency
  • Reduces heart failure symptoms
  • Can improve survival rates

Temporary Pacemakers

How They Work

Used for short-term situations where pacing is needed temporarily. Often placed through the skin into the heart without surgery.

Used For:

  • During heart surgery
  • Acute heart rhythm problems
  • Medication overdoses affecting heart rhythm

Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD)

How They Work

Combines pacemaker function with the ability to deliver a shock to stop dangerous, life-threatening arrhythmias like ventricular fibrillation.

Best For:

  • Patients at risk for sudden cardiac death
  • Patients with severe arrhythmias

Advantages:

  • Detects and stops life-threatening rhythms
  • Can deliver therapy automatically

Leadless Pacemakers

How They Work

A newer innovation—a pacemaker with no leads, placed directly in the heart. Completely contained and requires no external wires.

Advantages:

  • Minimally invasive procedure
  • No lead complications
  • Lower infection risk
  • Faster recovery
